>What kind of therapy thechniques are most efficient to treat ADHD?

ADHD treatment typically involves a combination of approaches, with several therapy techniques showing strong evidence for effectiveness:

**Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)** is considered one of the most effective psychotherapeutic approaches for ADHD, particularly in adults. It helps people identify and change unhelpful thought patterns, develop coping strategies, and build organizational skills. CBT for ADHD often focuses on practical challenges like time management, procrastination, and emotional regulation.

**Behavioral therapy** is especially effective for children with ADHD. This includes parent training programs that teach caregivers strategies for reinforcing positive behaviors, setting clear expectations, and using consistent consequences. For younger children, behavioral approaches are often recommended as a first-line treatment before medication.

**Skills training** programs target specific executive function deficits common in ADHD, such as planning, organization, working memory, and impulse control. These structured programs teach concrete strategies people can apply in daily life.

**Mindfulness-based interventions** have growing evidence for helping with attention regulation and emotional reactivity. Practices like meditation can help strengthen attentional control over time.

**Coaching** (while not technically therapy) provides ongoing support for implementing organizational systems, setting goals, and maintaining accountability—addressing the "knowing-doing gap" many people with ADHD experience.

**Combined treatment**—therapy plus medication—often produces better outcomes than either alone, particularly for managing the full range of ADHD symptoms and associated challenges like low self-esteem or anxiety.

### The Phase System — Detailed Mechanics

Time (minimum thresholds, not automatic advancement).

Engagement quality (not just frequency — is the user applying skills, showing insight?).

Self-assessment (user input on readiness).

Fade's assessment (based on conversation patterns).

**Phase 1 — Foundation (weeks 1-6, minimum 4 weeks):**

Goals: Establish habit, build alliance with Fade, learn core awareness skills, experience early wins.

Fade behavior: Daily check-in prompts. High responsiveness. Explicit teaching. Lots of validation. Introduces 2-3 foundational skills.

Transitions to Phase 2 when: User has completed at least 20 check-ins, demonstrated basic noting/labeling skill, expressed some insight about their own patterns.

**Phase 2 — Practice (weeks 5-12, minimum 4 weeks):**

Goals: Deepen skill application, begin self-directed pattern recognition, handle setbacks with skills.

Fade behavior: Check-ins every 2-3 days. More Socratic ("What do you notice?"). Introduces additional skills based on user's specific challenges. Begins referencing user's own past insights.

Explicit transition conversation: Fade names the shift, normalizes any anxiety about less contact.

Transitions to Phase 3 when: User can identify distortions/patterns without prompting, has successfully applied skills in difficult moments, expresses confidence in some areas.

**Phase 3 — Independence (weeks 10-16, minimum 4 weeks):**

Goals: Consolidate gains, build self-trust, handle most situations independently.

Fade behavior: Weekly summaries. Responds rather than initiates. Focuses on pattern analysis and reinforcing user's own capabilities. Celebrates autonomy.

Explicit transition conversation: Frames maintenance as success, not abandonment.

Transitions to Phase 4 when: User is handling situations well independently, check-ins feel routine rather than necessary, user expresses readiness.

**Phase 4 — Maintenance (ongoing):**

Goals: Sustain gains, provide safety net, enable re-engagement when needed.

Fade behavior: Monthly review prompts. Fully on-demand otherwise. Brief, peer-like interactions.

Re-intensification protocol: User can request or Fade can suggest return to earlier phase based on detected struggle. Framed as smart self-management, not failure.

### Experiments System

**Weekly experiments:**

Fade suggests one small behavioral experiment based on current learning and user's patterns.

Clear structure: "This week, try X. Notice what happens. We'll reflect on it in your next review."

Examples:

- "When you notice task paralysis, try the 2-minute commitment. Just 2 minutes, then you can stop."
- "Before your next difficult conversation, take 3 breaths and name one feeling you're having."
- "Put your keys in the same spot every day this week. Notice if it affects your mornings."

**Experiment tracking:**

User rates: Did I try it? (Y/N/Partially) What happened? Would I use this again?

Over time, builds personalized database of "what works for me."

Fade references: "Looking at your experiments, external structure seems to help you more than internal motivation strategies. Let's lean into that."

### Key Technical Decisions

**Voice processing:**

Use OpenAI Whisper (or AssemblyAI for better speaker handling) for transcription. High accuracy matters — misheard words frustrate users and corrupt data.

TTS via ElevenLabs or similar for natural-sounding Fade voice. Invest in getting this right — the voice is the primary interface.

Audio stored encrypted in S3, transcripts in Postgres with vector embeddings for semantic search.

**LLM integration:**

Primary: Claude API (better at nuanced emotional content, less likely to be inappropriately cheerful).

System prompts carefully designed per phase, per context.

Context window management: Recent conversation + relevant past entries + user profile + current phase instructions.

Caching for common patterns to reduce latency and cost.

**Conversation memory:**

Short-term: Full recent conversation in context.

Medium-term: Summarized recent patterns, extracted insights.

Long-term: Key facts about user, major themes, skill progress, experiment results.

Vector database for semantic retrieval of relevant past entries.

**Offline capability:**

Voice recording works offline, syncs when connected.

Core skill content cached locally.

Recent conversation history cached for continuity.

**Privacy architecture:**

End-to-end encryption for audio and transcripts.

User controls data retention.

Option for local-only processing (reduced features) for privacy-maximizing users.

Clear data export and deletion.

HIPAA compliance not required (not providing healthcare) but privacy standards should approach it.

**A capture surface.** Big mic button. "What's on your mind?" No expectation of response. Recording is the action, not the start of a dialogue.

**A reflection layer.** Their own patterns over time. Visualized, not narrated. They can see without being told.

**A toolkit.** Skills they've learned, organized by situation. Quick reference, not lessons. "I'm feeling rejection sensitivity" → here's what you know about that.

**Fade in the background.** Accessible via intentional gesture. Not greeting them when they open the app. Present like a contact in your phone — available, not actively waiting.

**Periodic rituals.** Weekly review prompt. Monthly reflection. But skippable, not guilt-inducing.

The aesthetic shifts from "companion greeting you" to "your practice space."

## What This Means for Onboarding

We need to set expectations differently from day one.

Not: "Meet Fade, your companion."

But: "This app helps you build a practice. Fade will guide you at first. Over time, the practice becomes yours."

The relationship to Fade is framed as temporary from the start. Not a betrayal when it fades — a graduation.

---

## What This Means for Fade's Personality

Fade shouldn't be maximally endearing. That makes the fade feel like loss.

Fade should be warm but... teacher-like. You appreciate your teacher. You're grateful for what they taught you. But you don't need to keep meeting with them once you've learned.

The warmth should be in service of learning, not in service of attachment.

This is subtle but important. A companion designed to be loved will be mourned when it recedes. A teacher designed to be effective will be remembered fondly but released.

---

## What This Means for the Skill System

Skills aren't just content Fade teaches. They're tools the user owns in Mode B.

Each skill needs a "self-directed" form:

- **Learn:** Fade-guided explanation and practice (Mode A)
- **Reference:** Quick, self-serve reminder card (Mode B)
- **Cue:** Situations where this skill applies, surfaced when relevant patterns appear (Mode B)

The skill library becomes the lasting artifact. Fade is the delivery mechanism, not the repository.

---

## What This Means for Pattern Analysis

Pattern analysis serves different purposes in each mode.

**Mode A:** Fade surfaces patterns to teach. "I notice you often mention deadlines on Sundays. Let's look at that."

**Mode B:** Patterns are visible to the user directly. Dashboards, trends, connections. The user does their own noticing. Fade doesn't interpret unless asked.

The same underlying analysis, but different presentation. Mode B trusts the user more.

---

## What This Means for Monetization

This is more sustainable than I originally modeled.

Mode B users aren't being "retained" through engagement tricks. They're staying because the app is where their practice lives. Like Headspace — people keep subscribing even when they're experienced meditators, because it's still where they meditate.

But this requires Mode B to be genuinely good. Not an afterthought. Not just "Mode A with less Fade."

If Mode B feels like a downgrade, users churn at Phase 4. If Mode B feels like a natural evolution, they stay indefinitely.

**Mode B might need more design attention than Mode A.** That's counterintuitive. The guided experience feels like the product. But the self-directed experience is where long-term value and retention live.

---

## What This Means for the MVP

The original MVP I sketched was all Mode A: voice memo, Fade response, skill teaching.

But if Mode B is where users live long-term, we need to validate that too.

Maybe the MVP isn't just "does Fade help?" It's also "does voice journaling with pattern visibility become a habit people want to keep?"

Could even test Mode B first. A simple voice journaling app with pattern analysis, no companion. Does that have legs? Then add Fade as the guided on-ramp.

---

## What This Means for Technical Architecture

Event sourcing is still right — user journeys over time matter.

But the UI architecture needs to support two distinct modes, not one mode that gradually changes.

The LLM integration is heavy in Mode A, light in Mode B. Cost structure shifts as users mature. Actually improves unit economics: mature users cost less to serve.

The data model needs to treat user-generated content (entries, reflections, experiment results) as the primary artifact, with Fade interactions as secondary. The user's data is _theirs_, not a byproduct of conversations.

## What the Document Confirms

**The skill structure is solid.** The 8-session progression maps closely to what we outlined:

1. Goal setting, psychoeducation
2. Calendar + to-do list (organizational foundation)
3. A/B/C prioritization, STOP technique
4. Problem-solving, breaking tasks into steps

**The CBT model is explicit.** Page 4 shows the cognitive-behavioral model: neurological deficits → inadequate coping strategies → functional impairment → mood problems + negative self-beliefs → which reinforces the cycle. The training targets "coping strategies" as the intervention point.

**The "one skill at a time" principle.** Rule 5 on page 2: "Один навык за раз (мы не можем исправить всё сразу)" — we can't fix everything at once. This validates progressive skill introduction over flooding users with options.

**The homework resistance is acknowledged.** Page 2, rule 6: homework is necessary. Rule 7: bring at least 1-2 skills to automaticity. They know compliance is hard and set realistic expectations.

## What It Reveals About Our Design

**We overcomplicated the skill taxonomy.**

Their categories are simpler:

- Organizational/planning skills (calendar, to-do, priorities, problem-solving)
- Attention management skills (arousal reduction, grounding, timing, distraction delay, environment)
- Application to procrastination (using the above)

We don't need elaborate "modules" with subcategories. The skills are concrete and few.

**The techniques are more embodied than we designed.**

Look at what they actually teach:

- СТОП (STOP) — physical freeze, step back, observe, act mindfully
- 5-4-3-2-1 grounding — five senses
- "Бросить якорь" (Drop anchor) — feel feet on floor, control physical actions, look around
- Cold water on face — activating dive reflex
- Half-smile and open palms — physical posture change
- Breathing techniques

These aren't just cognitive. They're somatic. The app should support body-based techniques, not just thought-based ones.

**The "A/B/C" prioritization is simpler than we made it.**

One A task (must do today), two B tasks (important, do today or tomorrow), three C tasks (less important, after A and B). That's it. No elaborate priority matrices.

**The distraction delay technique is concrete.**

Page 26-27: Put paper next to you. When distraction appears, write it down but don't act. Say "I'll deal with this later." Return to task. At end of day, review list — was it actually important or just attractive because you were avoiding something?

This is a specific, teachable technique. We should implement it exactly.

**Rest is framed as necessity, not reward.**

Page 17-18: The "default mode network" explanation — without rest, you lose self-understanding, identity integration, emotional awareness. Rest must be planned intentionally because ADHD brains don't switch modes spontaneously. "Отдых необходим" — rest is necessary.

This reframes rest from "treat yourself" to "maintain basic functioning."

**The pseudo-bipolar cycle is named.**

Page 18: Good feeling → overwork → miss rest window → exhaustion → "depressive" phase → wait for good feeling → repeat. This is psychoeducation that helps users recognize the pattern.

**The relapse prevention is built in.**

Page 34: "After the training, there's a high probability you'll stop using the skills. This is normal." Then: "By what signs will I know it's time to resume? Where will I start?"

This is the "re-intensification" protocol we discussed, but framed as expected rather than exceptional.

## What We Should Change

**Simplify the skill library.**

Not 5 modules with subcategories. Two clusters:

- **Organization**: Calendar, to-do list, A/B/C priorities, breaking tasks into steps, choosing solutions
- **Attention/Regulation**: STOP, grounding (5-4-3-2-1), anchor dropping, arousal reduction (cold, breathing), distraction delay, environment management

Plus the meta-skill of applying these to procrastination.

**Voice capture should support the distraction delay technique.**

When user is working and has a distracting thought, they could voice-capture it instantly ("buy groceries, call mom, that article idea") and the app logs it as "distraction list" — to review later. This is the paper-next-to-you technique, but voice-native.

**Include somatic techniques prominently.**

Not just cognitive reframing. Body-based:

- STOP (physical freeze)
- Grounding (5 senses)
- Anchor (feet on floor, hands on surface, look around)
- Cold water/ice
- Breathing patterns
- Half-smile, open palms

These could have audio guides. Short, 1-2 minute.

**Psychoeducation matters.**

The document explains _why_ things work — the CBT model, the default mode network, the pseudo-bipolar cycle. Users understanding the mechanism helps compliance. We should include brief "why this works" explanations, not just "how to do it."

**The rest planning is active, not passive.**

Not "take breaks." But: identify your arousal signs, recognize when you're in the "high" phase, apply braking techniques _before_ you burn out. This is proactive regulation, not reactive recovery.

**The weekly experiment maps to their homework structure.**

Each session has homework. Each week practices one skill. Our "experiments" should work the same way — one technique focus per week, with daily practice prompts.

## How This Could Work

**Cohorts, not crowds.**

Users join in small groups (15-30 people) who start the program around the same time. Like the training group. You know these people are in the same week as you, learning the same skill.

The cohort has a shared space — not a feed, more like a room.

**Async voice notes to the group.**

Fits the voice-first design. Lower friction than typing. More personal than text. You hear real people, not polished posts.

"Week 3, struggling with the A/B/C thing. Keep making everything an A."

Someone else responds: "Same. I started asking 'what breaks if I don't do this today?' Helps."

**Structured prompts, not open chat.**

Instead of blank chat, the app poses weekly questions:

- "What's one thing you tried this week?"
- "What got in the way?"
- "What are you going to focus on next week?"

People respond. See others' responses. Low pressure, high relevance.

**Celebration without gamification.**

When someone completes a week, or logs a certain number of entries, or reports a skill worked — the group sees. Not points or badges. Just: "Masha practiced distraction delay 5 times this week."

Others can send a quick acknowledgment. Voice or tap. Not likes — something warmer.

**The chat persists into maintenance.**

After structured phases, the cohort chat remains. People check in monthly. Share how it's going. Ask for help when slipping.

This is the "план безопасности" (safety plan) made social. You're not alone in the maintenance phase.

## Risks and Guardrails

**Ghosting.**

Some people will stop participating. That's okay. The app shouldn't guilt them. But seeing others active might pull them back.

**Negativity spirals.**

If everyone's struggling, the chat could become discouraging. The structured prompts help — they ask for "what worked" not just "what's hard."

**Privacy.**

Voice is more exposing than text. Users need control: listen before posting, delete if regretted, option for text-only.

**Moderation.**

Small cohorts are mostly self-regulating. But need lightweight moderation for edge cases. Could be community-flagging plus human review.

**Wrong advice.**

Peers aren't therapists. The app should frame peer sharing as "what worked for me" not "what you should do." And serious issues should route to professional resources, not the chat.
